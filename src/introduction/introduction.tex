This thesis aims on developing a neural network model to solve an inverse problem posed by the advection-diffusion partial differential equation (PDE).
The model will be applied together with Kalman filter to produce probabilistic forecasts in time with meteorological spatiotemporal data, i.e.\ data with space and time dependencies.

Inverse problems is a wide field of mathematics bringing together theoretical research and the wide array of applications where the theory is used.
Meteorological forecasting is a prime example of inverse problems where parameters for some physical processes can be solved from atmospheric measurements and used to forecast some weather conditions in the future.

Inverse problems are deeply linked to the well-posedness of a problem.
Jacques Salomon Hadamard (1865-1963) is said to have stated the three properties of a well-posed problem.
A solution of a well-posed problem has to exists, be unique, and depend continuously on the data.
In practice, many, if not most, probems lie in the complement of the well-posed problems which if also the case with the problem studied in this thesis.

In recent years due to advances in computing power and methodology, neural networks have been proposed as as the method to solve many kinds of inverse problems.
In meteorological spatiotemporal forecasting, the so-called physics-free models have been extensively researched.~\cite{rainnet, metnet}
However, in many cases it would be useful to constrain these methods to adhere to some physical models that can be written explicitly as PDEs.
This way when the model is used as a forecasting tool, the forecasted future states could be thought to represent physically possible outcomes in the sense of the chosen PDE constraints.
Moreover, this approach of modelling a system through the PDE modelling would at least in principle enable training a universal model for all data that can be modelled using the chosen equation.

But here lies the ill-posed nature of the real world problems.
Measurements are often incomplete, the simple PDEs that can be used here are merely rough simplifications of the true processes behind real measurements, etc.
So it is easy to see that the Hadamard conditions are hard to meet.
The possible solution is quite unlikely to be unique, and most likely doesn't depend continuously on the data.
But it doesn't mean the ill-posed problem should be ignored.
The simplified physical model and incomplete measurements definitely contain some useful information that can be used to recover a best possible solution in some metric.

Thus in practice, it is also vital to understand uncertainty related to the model outputs.
The classical Kalman filter from the 1960s is employed therefore to capture uncertainty in the forecasts the neural network produces.
